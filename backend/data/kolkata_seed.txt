Kolkata layered walking context (student-travel hero city):

New Market (Sir Stuart Hogg Market) — built in 1874, a Victorian covered market that became the lungs of central Kolkata shopping. Lanes mix sari sellers, spice heaps, and century-old confectioners; the “cultural intersection” feeling comes from communities that traded here for generations.

College Street — the world’s largest second-hand book corridor, lined with publishers and adda culture. Nearby is the Indian Coffee House, a student and intellectual hangout since the 1940s, layered with political and literary history.

Kumartuli — narrow lanes where artisans sculpt Durga idols from clay and straw; best visited before autumn festivals when workshops are busiest. The craft is both sacred art and neighborhood economy.

Bow Barracks — a tight Anglo-Indian housing enclave with red brick and balconies, a living remnant of colonial military families; easy to pair with a Chinatown lane walk for contrast.

Prinsep Ghat — riverside Neoclassical colonnade (1841), popular for sunsets and slow walks along the Hooghly; pairs with street snacks from nearby stalls.

South Park Street Cemetery — Gothic tombs in a quiet park; a meditative stop contrasting Kolkata’s noise, good for “hidden gem” storytelling on colonial migration and trade.
